# Source Quality Rubric

Use this rubric to decide how much weight to give a source.

## Tier 1 — Strongest evidence

Use as primary grounding whenever possible.

- official product documentation
- official vendor docs
- official repositories from the owner
- standards/specification pages
- direct engineering writeups by the organization that built the system

Questions:
- Is this the system owner speaking?
- Is the claim descriptive, technical, and specific?
- Is it documenting the real product surface or just marketing it?

## Tier 2 — Strong but not canonical

Useful for implementation reality and best practices.

- official repositories from recognized vendor teams
- technical guides from reputable software companies
- high-quality engineering blogs with concrete examples
- product/vendor skills published by recognized teams

Questions:
- Does the author have direct operational experience?
- Is the material concrete and reproducible?
- Is it consistent with Tier 1 evidence?

## Tier 3 — Good signal, limited authority

Useful for pattern detection, not final proof.

- curated GitHub lists
- community-maintained collections
- independent analyses that cite sources well
- visible ecosystem projects with active maintenance

Questions:
- Is it curated or just aggregated?
- Are examples concrete?
- Are claims evidence-backed or mostly promotional?

## Tier 4 — Weak signal

Use only as supporting context and label clearly.

- viral posts
- social threads
- forum discussion
- sensational summaries
- SEO-heavy recap sites

Questions:
- Can any important claim be verified elsewhere?
- Is this useful as market signal only?

## Claim labeling

When synthesizing, mentally sort claims into:

- **Verified** — directly supported by a strong source
- **Well-supported** — repeated across multiple decent sources
- **Plausible** — consistent with evidence but not directly proven
- **Speculative** — interesting, but not sufficiently verified

## Practical rule

Do not let a Tier 4 source override a Tier 1 or Tier 2 source.
